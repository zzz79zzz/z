ANSWER — Correct.

Metoclopramide , preferably as a liquid formulation, is the treatment of choice for diabetic gastroparesis. It is reasonable to proceed directly to a therapeutic trial in a patient with diabetes and suspected gastroparesis who has typical clinical features, in whom intestinal obstruction has been ruled out and other common causes of upper gastrointestinal symptoms have been considered and in whom other features of diabetic microvascular disease are present.

If the patient responds to treatment, this confirms the diagnosis. It is usually helpful to recommend a diet of multiple small meals, soft solids, and low fat without undigestible fiber to maintain nutrition until the pharmacologic treatment starts to work, a process that may take several days to several weeks.

Several tests are recommended for the evaluation of the patient suspected of having diabetic gastroparesis, including radionuclide gastric emptying studies, radiolabeled breath tests, and wireless motility capsules. The availability and use of these procedures vary from one institution to another, although a consensus recommendation has been published [ 1 ]. While studies using optimal gastric emptying test methods demonstrate significant associations between abnormal results and symptoms, the recommended tests are complex and difficult to execute properly. The available studies are insufficient to provide recommendations for patients with diabetes [ 2 ].

In patients with normal kidney function, one would ordinarily start with a metoclopramide dose of 5 mg by mouth four times a day (15 minutes before meals and at bedtime) preferably as a liquid formulation. The patient under consideration has kidney insufficiency. His estimated creatinine clearance based on his serum creatinine level, height, and weight is 28.5 mL/minute. At a creatinine clearance of <40 mL/min, 50 percent of the usual metoclopramide dose should be used. One should avoid treatment with metoclopramide for more than 12 weeks because of the increased risk of developing tardive dyskinesia with longer-term use.

Evaluation and management of this patient is discussed in the comment card. (See "Interactive diabetes case 13: Deterioration of glycemia in a 59-year-old patient with type 2 diabetes complicated by retinopathy, nephropathy, and neuropathy – Comment" .)
