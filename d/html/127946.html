Adverse Reactions (Significant): Considerations
GI toxicity

Tucatinib may commonly cause diarrhea, including severe diarrhea accompanied by dehydration, hypotension, acute kidney injury, and may be fatal. While grade 1 and grade 2 occurred in the majority of patients (Ref), grade 4 diarrhea was considered a contributing factor in patients who subsequently died. Mild cases of nausea, vomiting, and stomatitis have also occurred. Diarrhea, nausea, and vomiting were the most common serious adverse reactions in patients ≥65 years of age.

Mechanism: Dose-related; exact mechanism is not established.

Onset: Intermediate; median time to onset of the first diarrhea episode was 12 days; median time to resolution was 8 days. In patients who utilized antidiarrheal medications, the median duration of use was 3 days per cycle (Ref).

Hepatotoxicity

May commonly cause hepatotoxicity, including severe hepatotoxicity with ≥ grade 3 increased serum alanine aminotransferase (ALT), increased serum aspartate aminotransferase (AST), and increased serum bilirubin. Most increases to serum ALT and increased serum AST levels were low grade, transient, and reversible (Ref).

Risk factors:

• Preexisting severe hepatic impairment

Contraindications

There are no contraindications listed in the manufacturer's US labeling.

Canadian labeling: Hypersensitivity to tucatinib or any component of the formulation.

Warnings/Precautions

Disease-related concerns:

• Serum creatinine increases: Tucatinib increases serum creatinine by inhibiting kidney tubular secretion, but without affecting glomerular filtration (Murthy 2020; Topletz-Erickson 2020). Serum creatinine elevations initially occurred within the first 3 weeks of treatment (and persisted throughout treatment) and were reversible upon completion of therapy. If persistent serum creatinine elevations are observed, consider alternative markers for kidney function evaluation.

Special populations:

• Older adult: The incidence of serious adverse reactions was higher in patients ≥65 years of age compared to younger patients.

Other warnings/precautions:

• Appropriate use: Select patients with colorectal cancer for treatment based on the presence of HER2 overexpression or gene amplification and RAS wild-type. Information on tests for the detection of mutations is available at http://www.fda.gov/CompanionDiagnostics. Patients with breast cancer should have HER2-positive tumors.

Use: Labeled Indications

Breast cancer, HER2-positive, advanced unresectable or metastatic: Treatment of advanced unresectable or metastatic human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2)-positive breast cancer (in combination with trastuzumab and capecitabine) in adults, including patients with brain metastases, who have received 1 or more prior anti-HER2-based regimens in the metastatic setting.

Colorectal cancer, RAS wild-type, HER2-positive, unresectable or metastatic: Treatment of RAS wild-type, HER2-positive unresectable or metastatic colorectal cancer (in combination with trastuzumab) that has progressed following treatment with fluoropyrimidine-, oxaliplatin-, and irinotecan-based chemotherapy in adults.

Medication Safety Issues
Sound-alike/look-alike issues:

Tucatinib may be confused with afatinib, dasatinib, lapatinib, neratinib, talazoparib, tofacitinib, trametinib.

High alert medication:

This medication is in a class the Institute for Safe Medication Practices included among its lists of drug classes that have a heightened risk of causing significant patient harm when used in error.

Reproductive Considerations

Evaluate pregnancy status prior to use in patients who could become pregnant.

Patients who could become pregnant should use effective contraception during tucatinib therapy and for 1 week after the last tucatinib dose. Patients with partners who could become pregnant should use effective contraception during tucatinib therapy and for 1 week after the last tucatinib dose.

Tucatinib is used in combination with trastuzumab and capecitabine. Also refer to Trastuzumab and Capecitabine monographs for additional information.

Pregnancy Considerations

Based on the mechanism of action and data from animal reproduction studies, in utero exposure to tucatinib may cause fetal harm.

Tucatinib is used in combination with trastuzumab and capecitabine. Also refer to Trastuzumab and Capecitabine monographs for additional information.

Breastfeeding Considerations

It is not known if tucatinib is present in breast milk.

Due to the potential for serious adverse reactions in the breastfed infant, breastfeeding is not recommended by the manufacturer during tucatinib treatment or for 1 week after the last tucatinib dose.

Tucatinib is used in combination with trastuzumab and capecitabine. Also refer to Trastuzumab and Capecitabine monographs for additional information.

Monitoring Parameters

HER2 positivity (breast cancer and colorectal cancer); RAS wild-type status (colorectal cancer). Monitor ALT, AST, and bilirubin (prior to tucatinib initiation, every 3 weeks during treatment, and as clinically indicated); kidney function (prior to treatment initiation) and as clinically necessary. Evaluate pregnancy status prior to treatment initiation (in patients who could become pregnant). Monitor for signs/symptoms of diarrhea. Monitor adherence.

The American Society of Clinical Oncology hepatitis B virus (HBV) screening and management provisional clinical opinion (ASCO [Hwang 2020]) recommends HBV screening with hepatitis B surface antigen, hepatitis B core antibody, total Ig or IgG, and antibody to hepatitis B surface antigen prior to beginning (or at the beginning of) systemic anticancer therapy; do not delay treatment for screening/results. Detection of chronic or past HBV infection requires a risk assessment to determine antiviral prophylaxis requirements, monitoring, and follow-up.

Cardiovascular monitoring: Comprehensive assessment prior to treatment including a history and physical examination, screening for cardiovascular disease risk factors such as hypertension, diabetes, dyslipidemia, obesity, and smoking (ASCO [Armenian 2017]; ESC [Lyon 2022]).

Mechanism of Action

Tucatinib is a tyrosine kinase inhibitor that is highly selective for the human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2) kinase domain, with minimal inhibition of epidermal growth factor receptor (Murthy 2020). Tucatinib inhibits HER2 and HER3 phosphorylation, resulting in downstream inhibition of MAPK and AKT signaling and cell proliferation; tucatinib demonstrated antitumor activity in HER2-expressing tumor cells and inhibited growth of HER2-expressing tumors. The combination of tucatinib plus trastuzumab exhibited increased antitumor activity compared to either medication alone.

Pharmacokinetics (Adult Data Unless Noted)

Distribution: Vd: 903 L (metastatic breast cancer); 829 L (metastatic colorectal cancer).

Protein binding: 97.1%.

Metabolism: Primarily via CYP2C8, and to a lesser extent via CYP3A.

Half-life elimination: ~12 hours (metastatic breast cancer); ~16 hours (metastatic colorectal cancer).

Time to peak: ~2 hours (range: 1 to 4 hours).

Excretion: Feces: ~86% (16% as unchanged drug); urine: 4.1%.

Clearance: 53 L/hour (metastatic breast cancer); 89 L/hour (metastatic colorectal cancer).

Pharmacokinetics: Additional Considerations (Adult Data Unless Noted)

Hepatic function impairment: Tucatinib AUC0-∞ was increased by 1.6 fold in subjects with severe (Child-Pugh class C) impairment, compared to subjects with normal hepatic function.
