INTRODUCTION — Kleine-Levin syndrome (KLS), also called recurrent hypersomnia or periodic hypersomnolence, is a rare sleep disorder characterized by recurrent episodes of severe hypersomnia associated with cognitive and behavioral disturbances such as confusion, derealization, apathy, compulsive eating, and hypersexuality. Episodes last a few days to several weeks and are separated by weeks or months of normal sleep and behavior.

KLS is categorized as a central disorder of hypersomnolence; other disorders in this category include narcolepsy type 1 and type 2 and idiopathic hypersomnia. (See "Classification of sleep disorders".)

EPIDEMIOLOGY — Kleine-Levin syndrome (KLS) is a rare disorder with an estimated prevalence of one to five cases per million population [1-7]. Patients with primary KLS can be found worldwide, with a higher prevalence among individuals with Ashkenazi Jewish ancestry in the United States. (See 'Etiology and pathogenesis' below.)

KLS is more common in males than females. In several large observational series, the proportion of males ranges from 60 to 87 percent [3-7].

ETIOLOGY AND PATHOGENESIS — The etiology of Kleine-Levin syndrome (KLS) is unknown. Although a psychological basis was considered most likely when the disorder was first described, contemporary hypotheses have focused primarily on genetic and immune/inflammatory-mediated causes. There is also an association between KLS and a history of birth and developmental abnormalities.

●Birth difficulties – An abnormal birth history (eg, long labor, hypoxia, premature or postmature birth) is reported by 25 percent of patients. Developmental delay or learning difficulties such as dyslexia, dysorthographia, or dyscalculia are found in an additional 15 percent of patients with KLS [4,7]. Overall, approximately one-third of patients with KLS have an abnormal birth or developmental history.

●Genetic factors – A genetic basis for the disorder has been suggested by the preponderance of cases in the Ashkenazi Jewish population, suggesting a founder effect, as well as the existence of familial clustering and monozygotic twins with the disorder [2,4,7-18].

In the largest multiplex family described, a father, three sons, and two daughters were affected [11]. Exome analysis identified a heterozygous missense variant in LMOD3, a gene implicated in brain development, as a candidate gene [19], but an association was not reproduced in another cohort. A large, worldwide study of 673 sporadic KLS cases has identified TRANK1 as a candidate gene when associated with birth difficulties and developmental delay [20]. The genetic data also suggest some association with circadian genes.

●Precipitating factors – A variety of precipitating factors have been reported in patients with KLS. Disease onset is often closely associated with one or more health events or exposures, such as infection (72 percent), alcohol ingestion (23 percent), sleep deprivation (22 percent), unusual stress (20 percent), and physical exertion (19 percent) [4]. Other precipitating factors may include substance use (eg, cannabis, cocaine, gamma-hydroxybutyrate), head trauma, and vaccinations [3-7,21-26].

●Infectious and immune-mediated theories – An infectious, inflammatory, or immune-mediated etiology has been hypothesized based on frequent reports of infection-like symptoms at disease onset, but no specific antigens or autoantibodies have yet been identified [27].

Focal viral encephalitis or postinfectious inflammatory or autoimmune encephalitis affecting specific areas of the brain (ie, thalamus-hypothalamus, frontal and temporal lobes) would be consistent with the nature of the disease. These structures have a pivotal role in the regulation of sleep, perception, mood, and impulse control, and KLS-like symptoms can occur in association with hypothalamic, frontal, and temporal lobe tumors [28,29]. However, evidence for such pathology in the brains of KLS patients remains elusive. (See 'Neuroimaging' below.)

In most cases of apparently infection-triggered KLS, symptoms of KLS occur between one and five days after the onset of fever. Agents responsible for the infection are rarely identified and highly variable, however, and the interval to episode onset is too short for most immune-mediated processes. Specific infections reported in association with KLS include Epstein-Barr virus, varicella zoster, herpes zoster, influenza A and B, adenovirus, enterovirus, H1N1, severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2), Salmonella typhi, and Streptococcus [3,4,6,7,26,30-35]. The heterogeneity of identified agents suggests no direct effect [3].

●Associated conditions – A wide variety of genetic disorders have been reported with increased frequency in patients with KLS, including Klinefelter syndrome, von Willebrand syndrome, polycystic kidneys, and autism spectrum disorders (table 1) [4,5,36,37].

●Neuropathologic findings – Neuropathologic studies of KLS are limited to four case reports. In two cases of primary KLS, the following findings were described:

•Severe inflammation of the thalamus and mild inflammation of the hypothalamus were described in a 46-year-old man who died from aspiration pneumonia during an attack of hyperphagia [38].

•A 17-year-old male with a classic form of KLS and autonomic dysfunction during attacks died from cardiopulmonary arrest during an attack [39]. The neuropathologic examination revealed mild depigmentation of the substantia nigra and locus coeruleus. The cortex, thalamus, and hypothalamus were normal.

In two other reported cases, symptoms of KLS appeared to be secondary to a paraneoplastic syndrome, with abnormalities in the hypothalamus, amygdala, and gray matter of the temporal lobes [40], or inflammatory encephalitis within the thalamus and hypothalamus [41].

CLINICAL FEATURES

Clinical presentation — The median age of onset of KLS is 16 years [4]. Approximately 80 percent of patients present during the second decade of life [3-5], with a range of 4 to 82 years [42,43]. Patients with late onset (>35 years) usually have typical symptoms but tend to have longer episodes [26,38,43-47], while childhood onset (<12 years) is associated with more frequent episodes [3,4,48-51]. In one large series, females were older at onset than males (18.5 versus 15.0 years), despite an earlier onset of puberty (11.8 versus 13.2 years) [4].

Symptom severity during each episode tends to progress rapidly, within hours to days, and symptoms last for a median of 10 days [3,4,8,52]. There is a broad range, however, and episodes can last for a few days in mild cases to several weeks or even months in more severe cases. Milder cases of KLS have one to three short episodes per year, but moderate cases can have monthly episodes of 7 to 10 days each [3,4,30].

Prolonged episodes (eg, four weeks or longer) have been described in approximately one-third of patients and are associated with higher levels of anxiety, agitation, and amnesia compared with shorter episodes [7]. In severe cases, patients can experience 40 to 80 episodes in a rapid succession, with a possible permanent impairment of attention and mood [3].

Cardinal symptoms — Episodic hypersomnia is the defining feature of KLS. Additional symptoms present in most patients during episodes of hypersomnia include cognitive disturbance, an altered perceptive state, referred to as derealization, severe apathy, and disinhibition.

Hypersomnia — Hypersomnia is a major clinical symptom of KLS and is mandatory for the diagnosis [53]. Hypersomnia as used here refers to an increase in actual sleep time and is distinct from symptoms of sleepiness, fatigue, or low energy. Sleep is enormously increased during episodes, with a median total sleep time of 18 hours per day [3,4], sometimes with loss of the normal circadian rhythm of sleep [8].

Most patients are difficult to awaken and report intense dreaming and hypnagogic hallucinations; sleep paralysis is uncommon. The need for sleep is so intense that patients may be found sleeping in the hallway outside a classroom or on the sidewalk [54-56]. Patients remain arousable, waking up spontaneously to void and eat, but are usually irritable or aggressive when awakened or prevented from sleeping.

There is frequently a brief overshoot of insomnia at the end of an episode. This insomnia is sometimes associated with euphoria [4,44,57,58]. The need for sleep and total sleep time tend to diminish during later episodes [7].

Cognitive disturbance — Cognitive disturbance is the second most common symptom after hypersomnia. Patients are slow to understand and respond to questions. They do not initiate spontaneous speech, and they answer with monosyllabic or short sentences [3,4].

Memory disturbances are common, and the majority of patients report incomplete recollection of episodes. Temporal and spatial disorientation is common. Eye-hand coordination may be slower. Simple gestures, such as finding one's key in a pocket, may be difficult. This may be due to abnormal perception of objects with touch (astereognosia).

Formal cognitive and memory tests have been rarely performed during a KLS episode, and their validity is questionable as patients can be uncooperative, irritable, sleepy, and inattentive [59-61].

In between episodes, most patients are described as completely normal. With formal testing, however, patients with KLS score lower than age-matched controls on nonverbal intelligence quotient (IQ), processing speed, attention, and episodic memory retrieval [26,29,59,62-65]. In one large study, episodic memory performance declined after a mean follow-up of 1.7 years, despite a decrease in yearly episode frequency [65].

Derealization — During episodes, sensations of unreality are common and are probably the most specific symptom of the syndrome [4]. Surroundings are seemingly wrong, distorted, or unreal. Perception of sight, sound, smell, taste, temperature, and pain can all feel abnormal or unreal. Objects and voices are perceived to be distant [49,66]. The symptom has been described by patients with KLS in various ways [8,67,68]:

●"Unpleasant perception, bizarre and wrong"

●"A nightmarish sense of the surroundings"

●"The feeling of being almost in a dream"

●"Feelings of depersonalization, anguish, and a belief of a split between mind and body"

In one cross-sectional series, derealization was both sensitive (100 percent of patients reported it) and specific, as it was not reported in patients with other sleep or psychiatric diseases [4].

Apathy — Apathy is one of the major and most frequent symptoms during KLS episodes, reported by more than 90 percent of patients, often to the point of frank abulia [7]. Patients and families report the absence of self-motivation for almost all activities, including reading, watching television, using cell phones, and social communication of any type. Approximately 80 percent of patients neglect their hygiene during episodes.

Disinhibition — Although hyperphagia and hypersexuality have traditionally been considered hallmark clinical features, these symptoms are more variable across patients and episodes than previously thought and are better viewed as expressions of general disinhibition.

●Change in eating behaviors — Most patients report abnormal eating during at least one episode. Approximately two-thirds of patients have hyperphagia (eating large amounts of food at each meal, also referred to as megaphagia). The disinhibited eating is often directed towards specific tastes (eg, sweet, salty, or sour), even if these are atypical food choices for the individual. Other patients would eat any and all food before them, behavior that is reminiscent of frontal lobe syndromes. There are reports of patients who stole food in shops or off the plates of other patients [56,69,70], who searched for food in waste baskets [56], or who stuffed food in their mouths with both hands [71].

Hyperphagia and inactivity often lead to striking weight gain: 4.6 kg/episode in one large study [4] and 3.2 to 13.6 kg/episode in another [3]. Notably, however, up to one-third of patients have decreased appetite or eat less during some episodes [4,6,47,72,73]. These patients usually have a more severe hypersomnia, compared with those with hyperphagia.

The eating habits of patients with KLS are distinct from those of patients with bulimia in that there are no periods with self-induced vomiting or other attempts to control weight.

●Hypersexuality — Approximately half of males and one-third of females have hypersexuality during at least one episode [4,6]. Hypersexuality occasionally occurs in prepubescent children [49,51,74].

Symptoms include increased masturbation and inappropriate sexual behaviors such as exposing or touching genitals, masturbating in the presence of parents/caregivers and clinicians, use of obscene language, or touching other people inappropriately [45]. Although hypersexuality is often emphasized as a key symptom of KLS, it likely represents just one aspect of disinhibition that is characteristic of KLS episodes. A less specific behavioral disinhibition is more common [4,6].

Others

●Mood disturbances – Approximately half of patients with KLS have depressed mood during episodes, a finding significantly more frequent in females [3,4]. Suicidal ideation has been reported in up to 18 percent of patients, often expressed as a desire to bring an end to the episode. Suicide attempts have been reported but appear to be rare, possibly because of severe apathy associated with episodes [44,75,76].

Patients are often anxious, scared of being left alone or of answering phone calls, or afraid of novelty in their environment. They usually refuse to visit friends or families or to receive visitors, mostly saying that they do not want to be seen in this state.

Irritability is present in almost all patients, especially when sleep or food intake is prohibited. Aggressive behavior, such as biting, spitting, throwing stones, or tantrums at school, is rare [47,54,56,68,77]. Some patients exhibit regressive behaviors such as using childish words and voice or asking a parent/caregiver to sleep nearby [4,78].

In most cases, mood disturbances resolve by the end of the episode, although in rare cases they persist longer. A period of hypomania is not uncommon at the conclusion of an episode, extending over a day or two [79,80].

●Hallucinations and delusions – One-third of patients report brief, elementary hallucinations (eg, seeing a snake near the bed, hearing their name, but no insulting content). Delusions of reference are also commonly reported (eg, a feeling of being scrutinized by others) [76]. In rare cases, persecutory delusions and delusions of grandeur are severe and lead to a misdiagnosis of schizophrenia. (See 'Differential diagnosis' below.)

●Compulsive behaviors – Compulsions are reported in approximately one-third of patients. Examples include inappropriate singing, tapping, clapping, compulsive writing on walls, stripping down wallpaper, pacing, wringing hands, and tearing out hair [17,54,60,67,71,81-86]. Some patients repeatedly listen to music or watch the same videos over and over again [4,6,60,83].

●Physical symptoms – Fever, photophobia, and headache were endorsed by half of patients in one retrospective series, without stiff neck [4].

In a series of 108 patients questioned retrospectively about autonomic symptoms during an episode, 46 percent recalled sweating, 24 percent reported hot flashes, and 18 percent had nausea [4]. There have been rare reports of flushed face [57], thermoregulatory changes [26], hyperventilation [87], short episodes of flushes, profuse sweating, excessive salivation, hypertension and tachycardia [46], and hypotension and bradycardia [39,47,83,88].

Menstrual recurrent hypersomnia — Menstrual recurrent hypersomnia (MRH) is a rare variant of recurrent hypersomnia [53], identified in less than 20 women so far [5]. A pathophysiologic association between MRH and KLS is supported by report of a family with a daughter with MRH and a son with KLS [12]. Moreover, some girls with typical KLS have most of their episodes (but not all) in association with menstruation, making the differentiation between typical KLS and MRH difficult [26,89]. As a consequence, the third edition of the International Classification of Sleep Disorders (ICSD-3) considers MRH as a variant of KLS [53].

Episodes of hypersomnia occur just before or during menstruation and are associated with compulsive eating (65 percent), depressed mood (35 percent), and sexual disinhibition (29 percent) [5]. Compared with typical KLS, episodes of menstrual recurrent hypersomnia are usually shorter (3 to 15 days), less frequently involve cognitive changes, and sometimes respond to treatment with combined estrogen-progestin oral contraceptives [5,90,91].

Laboratories and studies

●Cerebrospinal fluid – During and between episodes, the CSF in patients with KLS is normal. This includes both basic CSF parameters (eg, cell counts, glucose, total protein) as well as immunoelectrophoresis for oligoclonal bands [51,54,92,93]. No consistent alterations in neurotransmitter levels or dopamine and serotonin metabolism have been identified [38,39,50,94-96].

CSF levels of hypocretin-1 (orexin) are usually within normal range (above 110 pmol/L) during episodes [8,97-100], although they are one-third lower during symptomatic versus asymptomatic periods. Such levels do not explain the major hypersomnia during episodes but suggest a possible secondary role of hypocretin-1 in pathogenesis.

●Electroencephalography – Electroencephalogram (EEG) findings in patients with KLS are nonspecific. There is no evidence of seizure activity during episodes, even when sphenoid electrodes are placed. In 70 percent of patients who undergo EEG during episodes of hypersomnia, a nonspecific, diffuse slowing (7 to 8 Hz) of background EEG activity is observed [3]. Less often, low-frequency, high-amplitude waves (delta or theta) are observed, mainly in bilateral temporal or frontotemporal areas.

●Polysomnography – Polysomnography findings during an episode of hypersomnia are variable and may be affected by the duration of monitoring (ie, 12 versus 24 hours) and the timing of the study in relation to the episode and disease onset [1-3,101]. Total sleep time is generally increased, ranging from approximately 9 to 12 hours, but sleep structure is essentially normal [1,2].

Less consistent abnormalities that have been described in one or more studies include a mild reduction in slow-wave sleep during the first half of an episode, mild reduction in sleep efficiency due to frequent awakenings during non-rapid eye movement (NREM) stages 1 to 2, and reduced NREM stage 3 and 4 duration.

In 19 patients with KLS, approximately 40 percent of patients demonstrated a narcolepsy-like pattern, with two or more sleep-onset REM periods during multiple sleep latency test [101]. Reduced sleep latency is not a universal finding, however. In other cases, the duration of sleep stages during daytime sleep contains an excess of NREM stages 1 to 2 [3]. In 20 children with KLS, the amplitude and the stability of the circadian rest-active rhythms drastically decreased, as patients were lying down and inactive most of the day [102].

Of note, polysomnographic findings may vary according to the stage of the disease. In one study, sleep studies obtained later in the disease course demonstrated that patients spent three to nine hours per day in a state of withdrawal with their eyes closed and an EEG pattern that resembled their awake state [103].

Neuroimaging — Structural brain computed tomography (CT) and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) studies are normal in patients with KLS [1-3,104]. Although various minor abnormalities have been reported (eg, frontal cysts, local atrophy), they are likely incidental findings [64].

By contrast, functional and perfusion-based imaging studies are frequently abnormal in patients with KLS both during and between episodes. Case reports and small case series have described a variety of abnormalities on single-photon emission computed tomography (SPECT), positron emission tomography (PET), and functional MRI [6,59,104-106]. The most consistent finding in studies using visual or semiquantitative analysis is reduced temporal/frontotemporal lobe perfusion, found in approximately half of patients; the thalamus, parietotemporal junction, and association cortex are also commonly involved. During asymptomatic periods, hypometabolism in the posterior associative cortex and hippocampus on PET may emerge as a useful clinical marker during the early phase of illness [107].

Persistent hypoperfusion in the diencephalic and associative cortical area during asymptomatic periods [104] may be a marker of complex thalamocortical circuit dysfunction, compensated by supplemental activation of other areas [108]. The altered brain activation pattern in KLS patients has been accompanied by lower performance levels and longer reaction times [61,108-110].

CLINICAL COURSE — Episodes of Kleine-Levin syndrome (KLS) tend to decrease in frequency and intensity over time, especially prior to disease termination.

Disease duration from the first to the last episode is extremely variable, with a spectrum ranging from 6 months to 41 years [3]. In a series of 110 patients with KLS, the median disease duration was eight years [3]. In another survey of 108 new KLS cases, the mean disease duration was 14 years, and approximately 15 percent of patients had a disease course longer than 25 years [4]. Risk factors for a longer disease course were male sex, hypersexuality, and onset after age 20 years.

In between episodes, patients with KLS typically return to a normal baseline. However, functional imaging studies and long-term neuropsychological and psychiatric follow-up in a growing number of patients suggest that there may be mild, long-lasting memory and attention defects [3,62-64,108-113]. (See 'Cognitive disturbance' above and 'Neuroimaging' above.)

Patients are at risk for the development of psychiatric disorders, most commonly mood disorders (other than bipolar disorder) and anxiety. In a study of 115 patients with KLS, psychiatric disorders were diagnosed in 20 percent of patients over one to six years of follow-up [76]. Females and patients with a long disease course are at highest risk. It is not known whether emergent psychiatric disorders are the consequence of psychiatric symptoms during episodes or a result of difficulty adjusting to KLS.

DIAGNOSIS — The diagnosis of Kleine-Levin syndrome (KLS) is made based on the clinical features of episodic hypersomnia with typical cognitive and behavioral disturbances and exclusion of alternative psychiatric, neurologic, and toxic or metabolic etiologies.

Evaluation — KLS is a rare disorder that should be suspected in adolescents, particularly males, who present with episodic hypersomnia, cognitive disturbance, and behavioral changes with a return to baseline between attacks. (See 'Clinical features' above.)

Because of the rarity of the syndrome and the nonspecific nature of some of the symptoms, all patients should undergo intensive investigation to exclude alternative psychiatric, neurologic, and toxic or metabolic etiologies. (See 'Differential diagnosis' below.)

●History – The evaluation should include a comprehensive medical history from both the patient and household members for verification, since patients are commonly amnesic for episodes. Particular attention should be given to cognitive and behavioral changes associated with the episodes, provocative factors, prescribed medications, and drugs of abuse. Videos of the patient's behavior during episodes are useful. Sleep logs are also useful, supplemented by data from validated sleep/wake wearable devices when available.

●Physical examination – The physical examination should include detailed neurologic and psychiatric examinations. The physical examination is normal in patients with KLS, and there are no specific neurologic findings. Although some patients report fever, headache, and photophobia, nuchal rigidity or focal neurologic signs have not been observed during or in between episodes [3], and the presence of such abnormalities should prompt consideration of an alternative diagnosis such as meningitis or encephalitis.

●Brain imaging – Brain imaging with and without contrast should be obtained as part of the initial evaluation to exclude alternative structural etiologies. MRI is preferable to CT, based on the higher resolution and sensitivity of MRI for a variety of structural brain pathologies.

When possible, we obtain brain functional imaging using positron emission tomography (PET) during asymptomatic periods to look for hypometabolism in associative posterior cortex or hippocampus. This finding is present in approximately 70 percent of patients during asymptomatic periods and hence it has good sensitivity to support the diagnosis of KLS, especially during the first years of the disorder [107]. (See 'Neuroimaging' above.)

●EEG and polysomnography – An electroencephalogram (EEG) during an episode may be helpful if aspects of the history are suggestive of seizure and is supportive of KLS when focal or global EEG slowing is identified. Polysomnography performed during an episode may show increased total sleep time but is otherwise nonspecific; the purpose is primarily to exclude alternative sleep disorders that may explain or contribute to excessive daytime sleepiness. (See 'Laboratories and studies' above.)

●Laboratories – Additional laboratory testing should be tailored to the presenting symptoms and the differential diagnosis; there are no characteristic cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), blood, or urine abnormalities in patients with KLS. Tests to consider include lumbar puncture for CSF analysis (including neuronal antibodies); blood tests to rule out possible causes of metabolic encephalopathy such as complete blood count, blood urea nitrogen, creatinine, liver function tests, ammonia, lactic and pyruvic acids, amino acids, acylcarnitine, toxicology screen, lead level, vitamin B12, folate, thyroid-stimulating hormone, and antithyroglobulin; and urine for toxicology screen, organic acids, and porphobilinogen. Serologic markers for specific pathogens (eg, Lyme) should be considered in selected cases. (See 'Differential diagnosis' below.)

Diagnostic criteria — According to the third edition of the International Classification of Sleep Disorders, text revision (ICSD-3-TR), diagnostic criteria for KLS include all of the following (table 2) [53]:

●At least two episodes of excessive sleepiness and sleep duration, each lasting for two days to five weeks.

●Episodes recur at least once every 18 months and usually more than once a year.

●Normal or near-normal sleep and wakefulness, cognition, behavior, and mood between episodes, at least during the first years of the syndrome.

●At least one of the following during episodes: cognitive dysfunction, derealization, major apathy, or disinhibited behavior (such as hypersexuality or hyperphagia).

●Hypersomnolence and related symptoms are not better explained by another sleep disorder; other medical, neurologic, or psychiatric disorder (especially bipolar disorder); or use of drugs or medications.

Menstrual recurrent hypersomnia (MRH) is classified as a clinical subtype of KLS in which episodes are exclusively associated with menstruation [53]. (See 'Menstrual recurrent hypersomnia' above.)

Differential diagnosis — The differential diagnosis of KLS includes a variety of psychiatric, neurologic, and sleep disorders. Importantly, most of these are much more common than KLS and usually can be distinguished by clinical features, neuroimaging, polysomnography, and lumbar puncture.

●Psychiatric disorders — Psychiatric disorders are the major differential diagnosis for KLS. Specific disorders that should be considered include depression, bipolar disorder, psychogenic/somatic hypersomnia, and acute psychosis. Features that may help distinguish KLS from psychiatric disorders include the following:

•KLS patients generally do not report psychiatric symptoms between episodes and do not have significant family history for these disorders [4]. This contrasts with bipolar disorder, depression, or schizophrenia, in which symptoms are persistent and a family history is often present.

•Compared with psychiatric disorders, the onset and offset of symptoms in KLS are much more abrupt (few hours to one to two days), and the length of episodes is shorter and more stereotyped.

•Although hypersomnia may be reported by patients with depression, in the latter the symptom is more often increased time in bed (clinophilia) with depressed mentation rather than true hypersomnia (increased sleep). Severe cognitive disturbances when awake, including confusion and partial amnesia as described in KLS, are also a distinguishing feature from depression. (See 'Cognitive disturbance' above.)

●Neurologic disorders — In the midst of an episode, particularly if it is the first, numerous neurologic disorders may be considered on the differential diagnosis.

One or more of the cardinal symptoms of KLS could be caused by structural pathology in the hypothalamus, frontal and/or temporal lobes, such as tumors (eg, craniopharyngioma, glioma, pinealoma, meningioma, colloid cyst), stroke, or inflammation/demyelination (eg, infectious, autoimmune, or paraneoplastic encephalitis, acute disseminated encephalomyelitis, multiple sclerosis). In contrast with KLS, however, neurologic symptoms caused by all of these processes are persistent, and patients often have additional findings on neurologic examination. Cystic tumors or colloid cysts within the third ventricle, which may cause episodic symptoms due to a ball-valve mechanism of obstruction, can be distinguished from KLS due to additional symptoms of increased intracranial pressure (headache, nausea, vomiting).

The episodic nature of the symptoms in KLS may raise the possibility of epilepsy, particularly temporal lobe epilepsy or nonconvulsive status epilepticus. However, cardinal features of KLS, including prolonged periods (days) of derealization, hypersomnia, hyperphagia, and hypersexuality, are not typical for epilepsy. An EEG performed during an episode can often distinguish between KLS and epilepsy if the two cannot be distinguished clinically.

Patients with Lewy Body dementia may have fluctuations of alertness associated with behavioral disturbances, but they are much older than KLS patients, have permanent cognitive deficits, and often have associated Parkinsonism. (See "Clinical features and diagnosis of dementia with Lewy bodies".)

●Other sleep disorders — Excessive daytime sleepiness is also a major symptom of obstructive sleep apnea, delayed sleep phase syndrome, narcolepsy, and idiopathic hypersomnia. However, the clinical picture in all of these entities is neither episodic nor characterized by such severe cognitive or behavioral problems. Sleep time may mildly fluctuate in patients with these syndromes, however; hence, polysomnography is generally indicated in those with residual sleepiness during asymptomatic periods to exclude an alternative sleep disorder. (See "Approach to the patient with excessive daytime sleepiness", section on 'Initial evaluation'.)

●Metabolic encephalopathy or intoxication — Less common causes of recurrent hypersomnia are benzodiazepine intoxication, drug and alcohol overdoses or abuse, and recurrent metabolic encephalopathies due to hyperammonemia, aminoaciduria, or organic acidemia. A metabolic encephalopathy should be suspected in young children with developmental regression, failure to thrive, recurrent vomiting, and a positive family history of developmental delay or consanguinity. Some metabolic encephalopathies can produce increased intracranial pressure and acidosis (eg, papilledema, pyramidal signs, Kussmaul breathing), which should not be present in patients with KLS.

MANAGEMENT — The etiology of Kleine-Levin syndrome (KLS) is unknown, and treatment during episodes of hypersomnia is primarily supportive. Patients with severe anxiety and psychosis should be treated symptomatically with appropriate medications and screened for suicidal ideation.

For patients with a history of prolonged or frequent episodes, lithium is the best studied maintenance agent to reduce the frequency and duration of episodes [114,115]. In patients with previous long episodes (>30 days), a trial of intravenous (IV) glucocorticoids may be administered at the beginning of a new episode.

Supportive care during episodes — Our approach involves educating family members, caregivers, and occasionally teachers about the core symptoms of KLS and providing conditions so that patients will not injure themselves during episodes of hypersomnia [116].

The best course for most patients is to allow the patient to sleep in a safe and familiar environment, avoid driving, and be observed for medical and psychiatric complications. Patients are generally exhausted during attacks, and sleep is often the least harmful way to wait for the episode to end.

Anxiety, suicidal ideation, and psychosis — Patients with disabling anxiety, suicidality, and potentially dangerous delusions and behavioral disturbances should be evaluated by psychiatry clinicians to assess risk of harm to self and others; hospitalization is sometimes necessary to protect the patient.

Severe anxiety and psychosis associated with KLS episodes should be treated symptomatically. Anxiety can be intense during some episodes; in our practice, we use either hydroxyzine or a benzodiazepine as needed.

For patients with sustained and prominent psychosis, we suggest an atypical antipsychotic agent such as risperidone. Dosing should be individualized according to symptom severity, clinical response, and side effects. Most patients can be tapered off once symptoms improve.

Hypersomnia and apathy — In our experience, symptomatic therapies such as stimulants are ineffective for hypersomnia during KLS episodes, and many patients do not tolerate them due to excess anxiety. Therefore, we suggest not using stimulants and other medications in most patients with KLS. Most patients feel better by sleeping rather than by being artificially awakened by the stimulant, only to experience the other symptoms.

The evidence base for symptomatic therapies consists of case reports and retrospective, uncontrolled series, with no single agent showing consistent benefits [3-5,30,117-128]. Due to the unpredictable course of the disease and the paucity of consistent positive results, reports should be interpreted cautiously. Specifically:

●Antidepressants – In our experience, antidepressants have no effect on symptom severity or episode frequency.

●Stimulants – Stimulants (eg, methylphenidate) and nonamphetamine wakefulness promoting agents (eg, modafinil, armodafinil) may have modest benefits in select patients but they do not address more bothersome symptoms of derealization, apathy, and cognitive disturbance, and they often increase anxiety [3]. If a stimulant is used, patients should be monitored for potential toxicities and unintended side effects such as exacerbation of anxiety, hypersexuality, aggression, or hallucinations [129].

●Amantadine – Amantadine has dopaminergic, stimulant, and antiviral properties. Possible benefit in KLS was suggested by one observational study, in which 10 of 24 patients treated with amantadine on day 1 of an episode were reported to experience episode termination or improved cognitive symptoms [3]. In our experience with amantadine in more than 100 patients with KLS, however, this effect could not be replicated [7].

Other therapies that probably lack benefit in patients with KLS include flumazenil (a benzodiazepine receptor antagonist), electroconvulsive therapy, and insulin coma therapy [3,44,45,71,75,82,130]. Sodium oxybate, a metabolite of gamma amino butyric acid (GABA) that induces deep sedation and is used in patients with narcolepsy and idiopathic hypersomnia, has not been well studied in KLS. One case report described clinical improvement in a patient with KLS treated with sodium oxybate, but interpretation is difficult because there was no challenge of cessation and readministration [122].

Headache — Headaches can be problematic during episodes. Many patients respond to simple analgesics like acetaminophen. For patients who do respond, we have had anecdotal success with acetazolamide 250 to 500 mg per day.

Prolonged episodes — Up to one-third of patients have prolonged episodes (>30 days) that are disabling and tend to be associated with more anxiety and agitation than shorter episodes [7]. Based on the authors' experience, one option to consider in these patients is a trial of high-dose glucocorticoids at the time of the next episode. We prefer IV methylprednisolone 1 g daily for three consecutive days, beginning within 10 days of the onset of symptoms. When it is impossible to provide IV methylprednisolone, we use an oral taper (eg, prednisone 60 mg daily for two days, then 40 mg daily for two days, then 20 mg daily for two days). If glucocorticoid treatment is successful in shortening the duration or severity of the episode compared with prior episodes, we repeat with each future episode.

The potential benefit of glucocorticoids is supported by a retrospective study in which 26 patients with long episodes (>30 days) received IV methylprednisolone 1 g/day for three days during one to six episodes [131]. When each patient's preceding untreated episode duration was used as a comparison, 42 percent of patients experienced shorter episodes (by seven days or more) with steroid treatment. Receipt of steroids within the first 10 days of the onset of symptoms was associated with a greater likelihood of response (60 percent). Among a cohort of 48 contemporaneous patients with KLS who did not receive glucocorticoids and who were matched with treated patients by age, sex, disease duration, and number of episodes, the rate of spontaneous improvement of seven days or more from one episode to the next was only 10 percent. Steroids were well tolerated aside from transient insomnia and did not induce mania.

Although short-term, high-dose glucocorticoids are relatively safe and associated with few side effects in most patients, potential complications include hyperglycemia, increased susceptibility to infection, bone loss and fractures, and neuropsychiatric side effects. (See "Major adverse effects of systemic glucocorticoids".)

Prevention of new episodes

Avoid precipitating factors — In an effort to prevent new episodes, we advise patients avoid alcohol intake, use good hand hygiene, avoid direct contact with individuals who are infectious if possible, and sleep with regular schedules.

Frequent episodes: Role of lithium — There are no established therapies for episode prophylaxis in patients with KLS, but a variety of drugs with potential mood stabilizing effects (eg, lithium, antiepileptic drugs, other miscellaneous agents) have been explored. In our experience, lithium is the most likely to be beneficial in patients with frequent episodes (eg, 4 to 12 episodes per year). Use of lithium for KLS is endorsed by the American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM) guidelines based on low-quality evidence [114].

In two large systematic, retrospective collections of case series that together included 59 patients treated with lithium, 22 percent reported at least mild improvement in episode frequency, and 24 percent reported clear benefit [3,4]. In a larger single-center observational study of 130 patients with KLS regularly followed up, 37 percent of patients treated with lithium (26 out of 71) experienced complete resolution of episodes over a mean follow-up of 22 months, compared with 3 percent of contemporaneous patients who were not treated with lithium [132]. Another 46 percent of patients had a reduction of episode frequency, duration, or intensity, and 17 percent had no change of these markers using lithium ("lithium-resistant" patients). Ten percent of patients had no more full-blown episodes but rare "mini-KLS episodes" of half a day to one day when treated with lithium. The time spent incapacitated was one month lower per year in treated versus untreated patients. One or more side effects of lithium were reported by 50 percent of patients but were generally mild and led to treatment discontinuation in only 7 percent. The most common side effects were tremor, increased drinking, acne, diarrhea, and subclinical hypothyroidism (11 percent; only two patients required levothyroxine).

In various series, the effective dose range of lithium (slow-release form) is 600 to 2000 mg at bed time (to achieve serum levels of 0.8 to 1.2 mmol/L when collected 12 hours after intake). The required concentrations are in the recommended range for bipolar disorder. Higher doses of lithium can have serious side effects (eg, thyroid and renal toxicities), and the risk-to-benefit ratio should be reassessed every six months. In some reports, episodes have recurred after two days of missed doses of lithium, highlighting the potential importance of adherence to therapy and providing some evidence of benefit [116,132]. The pharmacology, administration, and side effects of lithium are reviewed in more detail elsewhere. (See "Bipolar disorder in adults and lithium: Pharmacology, administration, and management of adverse effects".)

More limited and less consistent results have been described with valproate and carbamazepine [3,4].

Residual symptoms in between episodes — Over time, some patients develop very mild residual symptoms during "asymptomatic" periods, including mild apathy or brief episodes of derealization, fatigue, residual sleepiness, and cognitive slowing. (See 'Clinical course' above.)

This stage of the disorder requires further study. In our experience, repeated courses of glucocorticoids do not alleviate these symptoms; in this way, the course bears similarity to the secondary progressive form of multiple sclerosis [131].

SUMMARY AND RECOMMENDATIONS

●Definition – Kleine-Levin syndrome (KLS) is a rare sleep disorder characterized by relapsing-remitting episodes of hypersomnia and cognitive disturbances, including apathy and derealization. KLS is a central disorder of hypersomnolence. (See 'Introduction' above and "Classification of sleep disorders".)

●Etiology – The etiology of KLS is unknown. Contemporary hypotheses have focused primarily on developmental and genetic risk factors and immune/inflammatory causes. (See 'Etiology and pathogenesis' above.)

●Clinical presentation – The median age of onset is 16 years, and there is a male predominance. Episodes tend to progress rapidly, within hours to days, and symptoms last for a median of 10 days. (See 'Epidemiology' above and 'Clinical presentation' above.)

•Cardinal symptoms – Episodic hypersomnia is a defining feature of KLS. Additional symptoms present in most patients during episodes of hypersomnia include cognitive disturbances, an altered perceptive state (derealization), and prominent apathy. Hyperphagia and hypersexuality are variably present. (See 'Cardinal symptoms' above.)

Additional symptoms that may be reported during episodes include mood disturbances, anxiety, hallucinations and delusions, and compulsive behaviors. (See 'Others' above.)

•Clinical course – In between episodes, most patients with KLS return to a normal baseline and are generally indistinguishable from controls on measures of sleep, cognition, and mood. Over time, patients are at increased risk for mild neurocognitive impairment and emergent psychiatric disorders. (See 'Clinical course' above.)

●Diagnosis – The diagnosis of KLS is made based on the clinical features of episodic hypersomnia with typical cognitive and behavioral disturbances (table 2) and exclusion of alternative psychiatric, neurologic, and toxic or metabolic etiologies. In between episodes, we suggest brain positron emission tomography (PET), where available, to assess for regional hypometabolism in the posterior associated cortex or hippocampus, which is supportive of the diagnosis of KLS. (See 'Evaluation' above and 'Diagnostic criteria' above.)

●Differential diagnosis – The differential diagnosis of KLS includes a variety of psychiatric, neurologic, and sleep disorders, most of which are much more common than KLS. Most can be distinguished by clinical features, structural neuroimaging, EEG, polysomnography, and lumbar puncture in selected cases. (See 'Differential diagnosis' above.)

●Management during episodes

•Nonpharmacologic management, which includes establishing a safe sleeping environment, educating caregivers and teachers, and monitoring for medical and psychiatric complications, is the mainstay of therapy for all patients. (See 'Supportive care during episodes' above.)

•Patients with disabling anxiety, suicidal ideation, or potentially dangerous delusions and behavioral disturbances should be evaluated by psychiatry clinicians to assess risk of harm to self and others; hospitalization is sometimes necessary. Severe anxiety and psychosis associated with KLS episodes should be treated symptomatically with benzodiazepines and antipsychotic agents, as appropriate. (See 'Anxiety, suicidal ideation, and psychosis' above.)

•We suggest not treating with stimulants (eg, methylphenidate) or nonamphetamine wakefulness promoting agents (eg, modafinil) (Grade 2C). Most patients feel better by sleeping, and stimulants are poorly tolerated due to increased anxiety. (See 'Hypersomnia and apathy' above.)

•For patients with a history of a prolonged episode (>30 days), we suggest a trial of high-dose glucocorticoids at the onset of the next episode (Grade 2C). If steroids shorten the duration or severity of the episode compared with prior episodes, we repeat with each future episode. (See 'Prolonged episodes' above.)

●Prevention of episodes

•We advise patients to avoid alcohol intake, use good hand hygiene, avoid direct contact with individuals who are infectious if possible, and sleep with regular schedules. (See 'Avoid precipitating factors' above.)

•For patients with frequent episodes, lithium, valproate and carbamazepine have been explored. Among these, for patients with frequent (eg, 4 to 12 per year), disabling, or prolonged episodes, we suggest treatment with lithium (Grade 2C). (See 'Frequent episodes: Role of lithium' above.)

●Residual symptoms in between episodes — 20 to 30 percent of patients with KLS develop mild cognitive impairment or psychiatric comorbidities that require appropriate assessment and care. (See 'Residual symptoms in between episodes' above.)
