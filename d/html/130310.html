Adverse Reactions

The following adverse drug reactions and incidences are derived from product labeling unless otherwise specified.

>10%: Gastrointestinal: Abdominal pain (10% to 23%), diarrhea (10% to 15%), vomiting (10% to 15%)

1% to 10%:

Gastrointestinal: Flatulence (6%), gastroesophageal reflux disease (5% to 10%)

Nervous system: Fatigue (6%), headache (9%)

Neuromuscular & skeletal: Back pain (2% to 10%)

<1%: Dermatologic: Maculopapular rash

Frequency not defined: Hepatic: Abnormal hepatic function tests (including increased serum alanine aminotransferase and increased serum aspartate aminotransferase)

Contraindications

There are no contraindications listed in the manufacturer's US labeling.

Canadian labeling: Hypersensitivity to berotralstat or any component of the formulation.

Warnings/Precautions

Concerns related to adverse effects:

• QT prolongation: Doses >150 mg have been associated with QT prolongation; avoid additional doses or doses >150 mg or use for acute attacks.

Use: Labeled Indications

Hereditary angioedema, prophylaxis: Prevention of attacks of hereditary angioedema (HAE) in adults and pediatric patients ≥12 years of age.

Limitations of use: Safety and efficacy have not been established for treatment of acute HAE attacks.

Medication Safety Issues
Sound-alike/look-alike issues:

Berotralstat may be confused with belinostat, vorinostat.

Metabolism/Transport Effects

Substrate of BCRP/ABCG2, CYP2D6 (minor), CYP3A4 (minor), P-glycoprotein/ABCB1 (major); Note: Assignment of Major/Minor substrate status based on clinically relevant drug interaction potential; Inhibits CYP2C19 (weak), CYP2C9 (weak), CYP2D6 (weak), CYP3A4 (moderate)

Pregnancy Considerations

Adverse effects were not observed in animal reproduction studies.

If prevention for hereditary angioedema (HAE) attacks is needed during pregnancy, agents other than berotralstat are recommended. Patients with HAE should be monitored closely during pregnancy and for at least 72 hours after delivery (WAO/EAACI [Maurer 2022]).

Breastfeeding Considerations

It is not known if berotralstat is present in breast milk.

According to the manufacturer, the decision to breastfeed during therapy should consider the risk of infant exposure, the benefits of breastfeeding to the infant, and the benefits of treatment to the mother. Lactation may increase the frequency of hereditary angioedema (HAE) attacks; patients with HAE should be monitored closely. If prevention for hereditary angioedema attacks is needed in breastfeeding patients, agents other than berotralstat are recommended (WAO/EAACI [Maurer 2022]).

Mechanism of Action

Berotralstat is a plasma kallikrein inhibitor that binds plasma kallikrein and inhibits its proteolytic activity, controlling excess bradykinin generation in patients with hereditary angioedema.

Pharmacokinetics (Adult Data Unless Noted)

Protein binding: ~99%.

Metabolism: Hepatic via CYP2D6 and CYP3A4.

Half-life elimination: ~93 hours (range: 39 to 152 hours).

Time to peak: 5 hours (range: 1 to 8 hours).

Excretion: Urine: ~9% (3.4% as unchanged drug); feces: 79%.

Pharmacokinetics: Additional Considerations (Adult Data Unless Noted)

Pediatric patients: Exposure in ages 12 to <18 years is 20% higher compared to adults; difference is not considered clinically meaningful.
