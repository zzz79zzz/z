Differential diagnosis of cyclic vomiting syndrome
Medications and toxic etiologies Disorders of the gut and peritoneum Neurologic causes Endocrinologic and metabolic causes Gynecologic or urologic disorders Miscellaneous causes
  • Cancer chemotherapy
  • Analgesics (eg, aspirin, NSAIDs, narcotics)
  • Cardiovascular medications and diuretics
  • Hormonal therapies (oral antidiabetic agents, contraceptives)
  • Antibiotics/antivirals
    • Erythromycin
    • Tetracycline
    • Sulfonamides
    • Antituberculous drugs
    • Acyclovir
  • CNS-active drugs (eg, antiparkinsonian drugs, antiseizure medications)
  • Antiasthmatics
  • Radiation therapy
  • Jamaican vomiting sickness (unripe ackee fruit)
  • Drug misuse and abuse
    • Cannabinoid hyperemesis syndrome
    • Ethanol abuse
    • Nicotine use
    • Hypervitaminosis A
    • Apomorphine poisoning (medical child abuse)
  • Mechanical obstruction
    • Gastric outlet obstruction
    • Malrotation with intermittent volvulus*

    • Median arcuate ligament syndrome
    • Duodenal atresia, web, or diverticulum
    • Other causes of small bowel obstruction
  • Functional gastrointestinal disorders
    • Nonulcer dyspepsia
    • Functional vomiting*
  • Dysmotility
    • Gastroparesis*
    • Chronic intestinal pseudo-obstruction
  • Peptic and inflammatory

    • Crohn disease

    • Mesenteric ischemia
    • Retroperitoneal fibrosis

    • Abdominal migraine*

  • Demyelinating disorders

    • Heart failure
    • Radiofrequency ablationΔ
  • Postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome (POTS)*
  • Starvation

NSAIDs: nonsteroidal antiinflammatory drugs; CNS: central nervous system.

* Indicates disorders that are more common considerations for children[1].

¶ Seizure disorders that may mimic cyclic vomiting syndrome include early-onset childhood occipital epilepsy (Panayiotopoulos syndrome) or temporal lobe epilepsy[2].

Δ Vomiting after radiofrequency ablation is very common but may be anesthesia related[3,4].
